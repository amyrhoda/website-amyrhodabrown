Editing and Custom Book Production

Levels of Editing

Every document is different — different writer, different audience, different voice — so editors offer various levels of editing. This article explains the levels of editing I offer.

If you're still not sure what level of editing you need after you read this, please contact me — I would be happy to review a few pages of your document and talk about what you're hoping for.

The community of editors has not yet settled on consistent names or definitions for each level of editing, so I've included some of the most common names. My definitions are pretty close to those used by the Editors' Association of Canada.

  1. Developmental Editing
  2. Stylistic Editing
  3. Copyediting
  4. Proofreading

1. Developmental (or Structural or Substantive) Editing

Developmental editing is the big-picture edit; we'll work together to assess the structure of your entire document at the chapter and paragraph level. What order should ideas be presented in? How long should the chapters be? Is your tone consistent and appropriate?

Developmental editing has to happen quite early in the process; it can be very discouraging to feel like your document is "just about done" and then realize that, say, the ideas really should be presented in the reverse order! But developmental editing can be a great option if you're "stuck" or don't understand why your work isn't coming together the way you feel it should.

When I do a developmental edit on your book, I'll read it with an eye to the big picture: Does the logic make sense? Are you explaining your ideas in the right order? Could we present the material in a better framework? Can we structure the material using a metaphor? Are transitions between sections awkward? Should we use sidebars, footnotes, or endnotes?

I'll also consider the tone of your writing. Is it written at the correct level? Is the writing consistent, or do you switch between casual language and a more academic style? Is it ever patronizing? Sarcastic? I'll give you suggestions to help smooth over changes in tone which might distract your reader.

When I have finished reviewing your document, I'll send it back to you with comments and notes for suggested revisions, as well as a report on the overall structure of the work.

Developmental editing does not include stylistic editing or copyediting.

For more information about developmental editing, have a look at Scott Norton's excellent book, Developmental Editing: A Handbook for Freelancers, Authors, and Publishers.

2. Stylistic (or Content or Heavy) Editing

Stylistic editing can be thought of as paragraph-level editing. I'll edit your document with a focus on clarity of logic, clarity of language, and sensible paragraph and sentence structure. I'll look out for vagueness, redundancy, inconsistency, and wordiness. I'll check consistency in headings and captions, and look over figures and tables for consistency, readability and correctness.

Stylistic editing includes copyediting.

3. Copyediting (or Light Editing)

Copyediting is the last stage of editing, and can be thought of as editing the "mechanics" of your work. I'll correct grammar, punctuation, and spelling. I'll look for misused words, and check for consistency in captions, headers, references, and citations. Copyediting adds the final polish to make your writing clear and readable.

4. Special Mention: Proofreading

The word "proofreading" is often used interchangably with "copyediting", but they are very different things. To proofread is to read a proof, which is to say the final pre-publication galley proof.

The point of proofreading isn't to find major grammatical errors or logic mistakes — it's far too late for that. A proofreader most often finds mistakes in typesetting: wrong page numbers, inconsistent heading design, incorrect indexing. (You can correct minor spelling and punctuation mistakes at the proofreading stage, so long as it doesn't affect where the page breaks are.)

I offer proofreading — in fact, it's one of my favourite jobs — but proofreading jobs usually come from other editors or publishers. That's because an editor should never proofread the work she's edited or typeset; by the time I've spent hours with your work I'm too familiar with it and risk missing things. (I am happy to help you find a proofreader for a work I've edited and/or typeset.)
